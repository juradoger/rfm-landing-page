About the Species & the Reserve

The Red-fronted Macaw (Ara rubrogenys) is an endemic species of Bolivia, listed as Critically Endangered due to illegal trafficking, habitat loss, and negative perception from local corn and peanut farmers. For over 15 years, Armonía has been working on its conservation, protecting its habitat in the inter-Andean valleys and promoting actions to ensure its survival.

Located in the Inter-Andean Dry Valleys in the municipality of Omereque, the Red-fronted Macaw Community Nature Reserve constitutes a key refuge for the conservation of this species. This area holds the largest known reproductive colony, highlighting its importance for long-term survival. The reserve also harbors a remarkable diversity of avifauna, with over 180 registered bird species, and protects other endemic species of the inter-Andean valleys, such as the Bolivian Blackbird and the Cliff Parakeet.

This conservation initiative emerged in 2006 thanks to a joint effort by the rural communities of San Carlos, Perereta, and Amaya, in collaboration with Asociación Armonía, aiming to protect the main nesting site of the Red-fronted Macaw. Since then, the reserve has consolidated itself as a community conservation model, where local participation is key to protecting the habitat and biodiversity. Through nature tourism, visitors have the opportunity to observe this emblematic species in its natural environment, simultaneously contributing to the economic development of local communities and the funding of conservation actions.

In 2021, an inter-institutional National Census identified around 160 nests and an estimated population of approximately 1,200 individuals living solely in four river basins: Caine, Mizque, Río Grande, and Pilcomayo. This growth suggests that conservation efforts have been effective, though the species remains Critically Endangered.

Our Key Regional Projects

Río Caine Project

In coordination with Torotoro National Park, this program benefits over 131 families across 13 communities. We promote sustainable alternatives to reduce pressure on the species, implementing 80 agroforestry plots, delivering 1,000 fruit trees and 350 kg of seeds, and supporting beekeeping to generate conservation-friendly income.

El Palmar Project

At the El Palmar Integrated Management Natural Area (ANMI), where the macaws nest in native Janchicoco palms, we have installed 75 artificial nest boxes. We've recorded 13 active natural nests and engage in large-scale habitat restoration by planting over 5,700 palm seeds and protecting 23 hectares of forest.

Environmental Education

Education is key to our strategy. Locally, we work with over 1,000 children and have trained 124 teachers in 28 schools. Nationally, our "Las Queremos Libres" campaign reached over 2 million people, successfully raising awareness about the harms of the illegal pet trade and the importance of keeping wildlife free.

Observation Hides built to honor the Red-fronted Macaw

Another one of our achievements: launching and establishing the Observation Hide in the Red-fronted Macaw Reserve.

It all began with a simple yet powerful vision

To create a space where people can observe the Red-fronted Macaw without disturbing its natural world. A quiet hide that blends into the landscape, letting visitors appreciate these birds just as they are.

Crafted for immersive viewing

Rustic style, approx. 4 × 3 meters and 2.20 meters high, with six small viewing windows aligned to favorite perching spots so every guest can capture a memorable photograph.

Built by skilled hands, aimed at lasting impact

A team of naturalistic sculptors brought it to life—positioning the Reserve as a high-quality birdwatching destination that supports and benefits local communities.

Conservation Action Plan

The Red-fronted Macaw Action Plan establishes strategic lines of work for the next ten years to reduce direct threats and promote sustainable development in communities across 13 key municipalities. Key initiatives include:

Habitat Restoration

We focus on recovering key ecosystems in the Community Reserve. Within an 8-hectare enclosed area protected from livestock, we are driving both natural and assisted regeneration using native species like chañarillo, t'jako, and soto to restore vital habitat.

Eco-agriculture & Peanuts

By promoting crop diversification and agroforestry plots, we reduce conflict between farmers and macaws. We also encourage traditional peanut farming where communities leave post-harvest stubble for the macaws, strengthening local economies and fostering coexistence.

Community Beekeeping

Beekeeping offers a sustainable economic alternative that regenerates native forests. We've produced the first Janchicoco honey and formed the first legally established beekeepers association in Potosí, projecting over 1,400 kg of honey production directly linked to conservation.

Nest Box Project

To increase reproductive opportunities, we've installed 75 nest boxes on palm trunks in El Palmar and 20 in the Community Reserve. These structures are continuously monitored with camera traps and dataloggers to evaluate and improve nesting conditions.
